How Gothic architecture and auditory manipulation destroy the human mind in Leroux's The Phantom of the Opera and Frictional Games's Amnesia: The Dark Descent

Primary: The Phantom of the Opera · Leroux, 1910 Primary: Amnesia: The Dark Descent · Frictional Games, 2010
Descend
Introduction

The Architecture of Fear

In Gaston Leroux's 1910 novel The Phantom of the Opera, the horror is not caused by any sort of curse or supernatural force. It is caused by the dark descent — both physically and mentally. Leroux takes the fancy, glamorous Paris Opera House and turns it into a maze that breaks the characters and strips away their control. He makes the connection between the physical descent into the deep cellars of the Opera House and the breakdown of the human mind.

One hundred years later, the Swedish game studio Frictional Games released Amnesia: The Dark Descent, a survival horror game set in the suffocating corridors of Castle Brennenburg. Like the Opera House, the castle is not merely a setting — it is a predator. It uses darkness, sound, and labyrinthine architecture to strip the player of their sanity just as the building strips Daniel of his.

Both texts deploy Gothic space and Gothic sound as instruments of psychological warfare. The descent into darkness — literal and figurative — is the mechanism through which architecture becomes a character, sound becomes a weapon, and the rational human mind collapses entirely.

This website explores three interconnected Gothic strategies that both works share: the labyrinthine architecture as an active villain, the use of sound and silence to manipulate emotion and sanity, and the inevitable psychological unraveling of anyone who descends far enough into the dark.

Page Two · Gothic Architecture

The Labyrinth as Villain

In order to appreciate the genuine horror of descending the Opera House, it is important to realize the stark contrast between its two levels. The Belle Époque period in Paris saw the Opera House as the epitome of wealth and artistic flair — a brightly lit world of luxury and high society. But Leroux quickly dispels the reader's illusions by showing it is built directly on top of a nightmare.

"The architecture of the building is very deep and disconcerting with seventeen stories, and more than three thousand doors." — Leroux, The Phantom of the Opera, ch. 11

The enormous architecture of the building is not meant to be awe-inspiring but is intended to be disconcerting and frightening. It becomes a trap where a person can get lost without leaving a single trace in a very public place. Leroux is sending a clear message: no matter how civilized and modern a society thinks it is, it is built on a primitive and savage foundation.

The Phantom of the Opera

The Paris Opera House

The underground lake is a dark, watery border between two worlds. When Erik transports his victims across this dark water in his small boat, the situation is reminiscent of crossing into the underworld in mythology — like the River Styx. Going from the bright, wealthy world of the opera house and crossing onto this lake is exactly like crossing from the land of the living.

Rather than a dungeon with iron bars, Leroux offers a contemporary, mechanical trap: hidden doors and disorienting dead ends. Erik's mirrored dungeon of torture creates fear not through physical pain but through the mind — hallucinations and artificial heat that feels like a desert, forcing victims to lose their grip on reality.

Amnesia: The Dark Descent

Castle Brennenburg

Castle Brennenburg mirrors the Opera House in its fundamental Gothic strategy. Like the Opera House, it is a vertical space — the player descends, level by level, deeper into the castle's bowels. The architecture was not designed for human habitation; it was designed to break the human mind.

In Amnesia, rooms shift behind you. Passages that should lead somewhere circle back on themselves. The game's design explicitly denies the player a map they can rely on, recreating in interactive form the same disorienting horror that Leroux creates for Raoul and the Persian when they descend below the stage.

Further Architectural Analysis

Leroux's underground lake functions as more than set-dressing — it is a mythological threshold. The Styx, in Greek mythology, is the river that separates the world of the living from the realm of the dead. It can only be crossed by boat, with the ferryman Charon as guide. Erik serves this role: he is the ferryman of his own underworld, transporting his victims from the rational, lit world above into the irrational darkness below. Once you cross, the rules of the world above no longer apply.

Castle Brennenburg employs a similar mythological structure. The player descends through increasingly infernal levels — wine cellar, sewers, prison, and finally the machine room and orb chamber at the absolute bottom. This vertical descent into darkness mirrors the classical katabasis (descent into the underworld) found in Dante, Virgil, and Homer. Both Leroux and Frictional Games are drawing on ancient mythological fear: the terror of going underground, of being below the world of the living.

The most disturbing space in Leroux's novel is not the lake or the cellars — it is the mirrored torture chamber. In this room, there is no fear of physical pain. The fear is entirely in the mind. The victim is locked in a room filled with mirrors that cause hallucinations and artificial heat that feels like a desert. The victim is forced to hallucinate and lose their grip on reality.

The Persian, the character who knows the most about Erik's traps, tells us that the dungeon is designed to drive the victim to the point where they will finally use the room's "iron tree, with its iron branch, for the hanged men" (Leroux, ch. 22). The architecture is not just a trap — it is a death machine designed specifically to destroy the mind before taking the life. This is the Gothic at its most sophisticated: the horror is not external but internal, generated by the space itself.

Amnesia deploys the same logic in its Machine Room sequences. The player is subjected to visual distortions — the game literally warps its own graphics to simulate Daniel's loss of sanity. Like the mirrored room, the castle turns your own perception against you.

Leroux was writing in the Belle Époque period — the "Beautiful Era" of French culture, a time of optimism, technological progress, and bourgeois elegance. The Paris Opéra (Palais Garnier, built 1861–1875) was a literal monument to this optimism: one of the grandest buildings on earth, a symbol of civilization's triumph.

Leroux's decision to place his horror beneath this specific building is a deliberate sociopolitical critique. The beautiful surface of the Belle Époque is built, literally, on a dark, mechanical, primitive foundation. The Opera House horror is a metaphor for the hidden violences of modern civilization — the workers, the marginalized, and the monstrous that polite society chooses not to see but which sustains everything above.

Amnesia's Alexander of Brennenburg similarly exploits his social position — a powerful nobleman — to conduct experiments that are hidden from the world above. The castle's architecture enables his crimes by concealing them in the depths.

Page Three · Gothic Sound

The Voice in the Dark

When you are in a dark place, underground, unable to see anything, your senses will be controlled by your fear of the unknown. Both Leroux and Frictional Games understood this intuitively: in the dark, sound becomes the primary sense — and therefore the primary weapon.

Amnesia: The Dark Descent — Sound Design Analysis

Amnesia's award-winning sound design is the core of its horror. Ambient groans, distant footsteps, and voices that seem to come from within the walls — the castle commands the walls just as Erik commands his walls. The sound directly affects the player's emotional state, which in turn affects Daniel's sanity meter.

Sound as Control

The Phantom of the Opera · Leroux

"He commands the walls."

— Leroux, ch. 19

Erik is a master ventriloquist who throws his voice to come from something solid — stone walls, ceiling, the floor. When you are in the dark, unable to see, and the walls themselves speak to you, your senses become weaponized against you. The Phantom does not need to be in the room. He is the room.

Amnesia: The Dark Descent · Frictional Games

"Something is wrong with this place. I can feel it."

— Daniel's journal, Amnesia

In Amnesia, the castle speaks through sound. Inhuman groans echo through corridors with no source. Footsteps approach from around corners where nothing is there. The game's sound engine was specifically designed so that players could not locate threats by sound alone — the castle controls what you hear.

Erik uses his role as the "Angel of Music" — the spiritual guardian Christine's late father promised to send from heaven — to completely take away any shred of her free will. Christine describes this awful feeling of losing her free will; Raoul responds with a chilling line:

"Oh, you are getting more like me now, seeing him everywhere!" — Raoul, in Leroux, ch. 13

The Phantom's voice has colonized Christine's mind to such a degree that Raoul — a rational, educated man — is himself beginning to succumb to the same paranoia. The disembodied voice in the dark does not just manipulate the direct target. It spreads, infecting everyone in proximity. This is exactly what Amnesia's sound design achieves: the player, sitting in their chair, begins to believe they hear footsteps that aren't there.

In Leroux's novel, the Phantom's opera Don Juan Triumphant is more than a creative work — it is a Gothic artifact. The composition is described as something alien and disturbing, music that bypasses rational faculties and operates directly on emotion and primal fear. It is the musical equivalent of the mirrored torture chamber: architecture built of sound rather than stone.

The climactic scene in which the Phantom takes over the performance of the opera — removing the singer and performing himself — is the apotheosis of auditory control. The audience cannot see who is singing. The voice comes from everywhere. The Phantom's genius is that he can make a public performance into his private weapon, controlling thousands of people through sound alone.

Both works understand that silence is its own form of auditory horror. In the Opera House's underground passages, Leroux describes moments of absolute silence that are more terrifying than any sound — because in silence, the imagination fills the void with worse horrors than anything real.

Amnesia exploits this masterfully. The game's most frightening moments are often the quiet ones: a room where the ambient groaning has stopped. The silence means something is near enough that it has stopped moving. The absence of sound becomes a signal of immediate danger. Both works use the structure of sound and silence as a rhythmic Gothic device — the alternation between the known and the unknown.

Page Four · Psychological Unraveling

The Mind Descending

The true horror of both works is not the Phantom or the monsters — it is the idea that any human mind, placed in the right conditions of darkness, disorientation, and auditory manipulation, will break. Leroux demonstrates this through Christine, Raoul, and the Persian. Frictional Games builds it directly into their mechanics through the sanity system.

The heroine of Leroux's novel, Christine, isn't afraid in the traditional Gothic sense — she is not afraid because she is locked in a physical prison. Christine is afraid because she does not know if she can trust her own brain. Her voice is being controlled by a voice in the dark. This proves that the darkest prison in the novel is her own trauma. This is the same crisis Amnesia's Daniel faces: he has erased his own memory. He cannot trust what he perceives or what he remembers.

Three Descents: Character Studies

Christine is completely locked in a mental prison long before she ever finds herself in a physical one. Leroux takes care to illustrate just how vulnerable Christine's character is after the death of her father. Due to how desperate she is for a source of comfort, Erik is able to manipulate her grief to his advantage.

Her breakdown is not dramatic — it is quiet and total. She stops questioning the voice. She stops testing reality. She simply accepts. This is Leroux's most disturbing portrait: not the mad scream of someone who knows they are losing their mind, but the peaceful acceptance of someone who has already lost it without realizing it.

The character of Vicomte de Chagny is a representation of the normal logical world. He is an educated man who is financially secure and believes in the rational world. But the moment he ventures into the underground to save the girl he loves, he loses all logic. The instant he is placed in the dark chamber of torture, he is driven mad by bewilderment and "unendurable" heat.

The Persian is even more telling. He knows more about Erik's traps than anyone — he has seen them before, understands how they work, can explain them rationally. Yet he too succumbs. His knowledge does not protect his mind. This is Leroux's most sophisticated point: intelligence and knowledge are not defenses against the darkness. The space destroys rational thought regardless of who the character is.

Daniel's situation in Amnesia is structurally identical to Christine's but experienced interactively. He has erased his own memory — a voluntary psychological self-destruction — to escape the knowledge of what he has done. This means the player experiences the castle without context, without memory, and without the tools to understand what is happening. The castle destroys Daniel's sanity not because he is weak, but because it is designed to destroy anyone.

The sanity mechanic makes the player feel this personally. When Daniel's sanity drops, the player's screen distorts, the controls become erratic, and Daniel begins to hallucinate. This is the interactive equivalent of Leroux describing Raoul as "raving" — the audience does not merely read about the breakdown, they experience it.

The Phantom · Erik

The Monster Who Is Just a Man

Erik is described like a walking corpse: "He is extraordinarily thin and his dress-coat hangs on a skeleton frame. His eyes are so deep that you can hardly see the fixed pupils. You just see two big black holes, as in a dead man's skull" (Leroux, ch. 1).

But the scariest moment in the novel is when he shouts at Christine: "I am neither an angel nor a genius nor a ghost... I am Erik!" (Leroux, ch. 12). This crazy, deadly man is just a normal man. That makes him more terrifying. He is a physical representation of what isolation and pain can do to a human being. He has learned to create hostile traps because society has always been hostile to him.

Amnesia · Alexander of Brennenburg

The Monster Behind the Architecture

Alexander is the architect of Daniel's suffering, just as Erik is the architect of the underground. But like Erik, Alexander is revealed to be a man driven to monstrous acts by circumstance — in his case, the desperate need to return to his home dimension and escape the Shadow that hunts him.

Both antagonists are masters of their environments. Both have created spaces that serve their pathological needs. And both are revealed, at their core, to be beings of profound loneliness — monsters made by isolation, not born into it.

Amnesia: The Dark Descent — All Three Endings

Amnesia's three endings mirror the Gothic tension between damnation, survival, and redemption that Leroux resolves through Christine's act of compassion. In Leroux's novel, the descent into madness is reversed not through cleverness but through human connection — Christine's compassion breaks Erik and ends the horror. Does Amnesia offer the same escape?

The Resolution: Compassion Against the Dark

The most remarkable thing about Leroux's novel is its ending. The characters never overcome their descent into madness through a clever trick or a fight with Erik. They overcome their psychological nightmare through a simple act of human understanding. When Christine stops running and shows Erik true compassion, the monster breaks down.

"I tore off my mask so as not to lose one of her tears... and she did not run away! ... She stayed, she let me kiss her!" — Erik to The Persian, Leroux, ch. 26

Erik then cries like a child, Christine calling him "poor unhappy Erik" (Leroux, ch. 26). The monster who was so masterful in his dark labyrinth is now a broken outsider who simply wanted human interaction — which he was denied his entire life. This ending demonstrates that the only way to survive in the madness of the dark is through emotional interaction. Gothic horror, in Leroux's vision, is cured not by heroism but by empathy.

Creator's Note

On Making This Project

This website is the final project for ENG 318: Gothic Literature and Games, Spring 2026. My original proposal was for a video presentation, but I switched to a website format to better realize the central themes of the project. The irony of studying auditory manipulation and Gothic space is that a website can demonstrate these things rather than merely describe them — you can hear the Phantom's voice, experience the distorted visuals of Amnesia's sanity system, and navigate the site's own labyrinthine structure.

My two primary texts are Gaston Leroux's The Phantom of the Opera (1910) and Frictional Games's Amnesia: The Dark Descent (2010). The comparison emerged from a simple observation: both works are obsessed with what happens to the human mind when it is placed in a Gothic space — underground, labyrinthine, dark, and filled with sound that cannot be trusted. The century between these two texts did not diminish the power of these Gothic strategies; it evolved them. Leroux describes the effect of the underground on the mind. Frictional Games makes you feel it.

The analytical work in this project draws primarily on three Gothic strategies: the use of architecture as an active villain rather than passive setting, the deployment of sound and silence as instruments of psychological control, and the inevitable mental collapse of any character placed in these conditions long enough. I have tried to demonstrate that these strategies operate identically across both texts despite the century and the medium that separates them, which suggests that Leroux identified something fundamental about how Gothic horror works — something that game designers rediscovered independently a hundred years later.

The most surprising thing I found in this analysis is the resolution. Both works suggest that the only escape from Gothic psychological horror is not intelligence, not bravery, but empathy. Christine's compassion for Erik dissolves the Gothic trap. In Amnesia, the "good" ending requires Daniel to choose mercy over self-preservation. Gothic horror, in both of these works, is ultimately a story about what happens to human beings when they are denied human connection — and what restores them when that connection is finally offered.
